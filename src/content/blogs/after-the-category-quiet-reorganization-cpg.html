I've spent seven years watching consumer brands change, and I don't really trust either of the two stories people tell about CPG. The first is that incumbents are finished. Too slow, too risk-averse, outflanked by some founder with a Shopify store. The second is that incumbents are fine, because distribution and capital still decide who wins on a shelf. Neither's right. What's actually happening sits between them, and it's quieter than either camp wants to admit. The categories that defined this industry for the better part of a century are dissolving into each other. Most of the org charts inside the big CPGs haven't caught up.

The CPGs navigating this well have figured out something that sounds obvious in retrospect but is honestly pretty hard to execute. Consumers don't organize their lives around product categories. They organize around identity, moments, and meaning. A Gen Z consumer who reaches for Olipop isn't buying soda. They're buying a gut-health-coded ritual that happens to be carbonated. On the same day, they might pick Celsius for energy, Liquid Death at the bar, Athletic Brewing at dinner, and Poppi on the way home, substituting freely across what the industry still calls four different categories. Previously, I spent some time in California recently and kept noticing the same thing at checkout: customers picking up a Poppi and a Liquid Death together, where ten years ago that same basket would've been a Pepsi and a Coke. The label is almost irrelevant to the purchase. Industry data shows functional beverages, a category that didn't even exist as a defined label five years ago, outpacing traditional carbonated soft drinks by three to five times. The labels CPG used to compete on (diet, regular, sparkling, energy) flatten into noise.

The shift is pressuring traditional companies whose organizational logic was built around a fixed category. R&D, the sales force, shelf negotiations, brand architecture; all of it assumes the category is stable. Consumers are quietly proving it isn't. The response across the larger players has taken two distinct forms, and understanding both, including why one has a much worse track record than the other, is the strategic story worth telling in CPG today.

Companies Are Reinventing Themselves From the Inside

The first group has looked at category instability and decided to bet on a new identity from the inside. This kind of organizational conviction is rare in large public companies. Why? Because you have to look at a legacy business generating most of your revenue and decide to move resources away from it, either because market forces demand it or because you expect them to.

Take Philip Morris International, where I've spent the last two years learning all I can. By the early 2010s, cigarettes were a textbook structural decline. Falling volumes, hardening generational stigma, and regulators tightening from every direction. PMI's response, ahead of its peers, was to redefine the business itself, not cigarettes, but broadly nicotine delivery. That reframe produced IQOS in 2014, a heated tobacco device that directly cannibalized the company's own cigarette volumes. ZYN came in via PMI's ~$16B acquisition of Swedish Match in 2022 (the largest deal in PMI's history), bought specifically to accelerate the nicotine-first vision. By FY2024, smoke-free products were ~40% of net revenue, and PMI's public commitment to making them a majority by 2030 still holds.

Take Mars next. At first read, Mars is best known for confectionery, Snickers, and M&Ms. In 2017, they spent $7.7B acquiring VCA, the largest veterinary hospital chain. Sounds weird, until you remember Mars has been in the pet food space for decades. Petcare is now Mars's largest segment by revenue, bigger than confectionery, a reorganization that happened largely without press coverage because Mars is privately held and doesn't have to perform against a public earnings cycle. The VCA acquisition wasn't diversification; it was vertical integration of an identity Mars had already quietly become.

Nestlé is doing the same thing, just more slowly. They've divested parts of their confectionery portfolio since 2018, sold US candy brands to Ferrero for ~$2.8B that year, and at the same time doubled down on coffee with Nespresso, Blue Bottle, and a health science division that deliberately sits between food and pharma. They're not becoming a pharma company. They're also not really a chocolate company anymore.

What these companies share is that they have named, explicitly to investors and internal stakeholders, what business they are in. It sounds simple, but it doesn't come easy.

Buying Their Way Into Relevance

The second group decided that the consumer trust they needed was faster to buy than to build. In retrospect, it makes sense. Niche brands spend years building authenticity, community, and cultural relevance in ways large CPG companies just can't replicate by design. That logic produced an entire decade of DTC brands being built and then being acquired by the larger players. One of the earliest I remember is P&G's acquisition of Native, an all-natural deodorant brand, in 2017 for ~$100M. CPG powerhouses buy the brand, and they buy the relationship, at least on paper. The larger company brings the distribution and capital. The acquired brand brings the identity the acquirer wants.

PepsiCo is executing the most disciplined version of this strategy I've seen. They bought SodaStream for $3.2B in 2018. They picked up Siete Foods, a grain-free Mexican-American food brand with Gen Z and millennial loyalty, for $1.2B in 2024. Then in 2025, they bought Poppi, a COVID-era prebiotic soda brand built almost entirely around gut health and Gen Z aesthetics, for ~$1.95B. Each one targets a consumer segment that Pepsi-Cola and Lays just can't build organically at the same speed. Each is a bet on real demand growth, not a trend.

Unilever and P&G chased the same strategy with mixed outcomes. Unilever bought Dollar Shave Club for $1B in 2016. Tatcha, Dermalogica, and Paula's Choice came after. Unilever's legacy brands had been slowly losing share for years. Native, as I mentioned, was bought by P&G. The intent is the same in every case. These brands had built something strong enough that the acquirer couldn't replicate it at the pace the market required, so they bought it instead.

And then something predictable happened.

Why Distribution Destroys What It Buys

The conventional wisdom on why CPG acquisitions of niche brands fail is mostly about culture and talent. The founder leaves. The team doesn't fit. Corporate processes slow everything down. Fine, fair, real pain points across M&A as a whole. But there's a deeper problem worth naming. The main advantage that a large CPG company brings to an acquisition is the exact thing that destroys what made the brand worth buying.

Large CPG players bring distribution and capital. Getting a brand into 100,000 doors in 18 months is a real advantage. Having a balance sheet to fund national media and weather bad quarters matters too. But the niche brand's value, the thing that justifies a 10x or 15x revenue multiple, was almost entirely a function of its inaccessibility, its specificity, its feeling of discovery. The person who found Native deodorant through a DTC channel in 2017 bought it partly because it wasn't sitting next to the Gillette. The day P&G pushed it to mass retail at scale, those consumers registered a shift. The product didn't change. The meaning did. And meaning, honestly, is most of what a niche brand ever was. (How niche brands scale without losing that meaning is a separate question, and a conversation for another article.)

Dollar Shave Club is the textbook example. Unilever paid $1B for what was, frankly, a media company that happened to make razors. Michael Dubin's original viral video wasn't marketing for a product; it was a product. Within about five years of the acquisition, Dubin was gone (often the story with M&A term clauses), the brand content had been sanitized into something indistinguishable from every other CPG campaign, and the subscription had stalled. Unilever sold DSC in 2023 to Nexus Capital Management for a fraction of what they paid. The brand still exists. What made it worth a billion doesn't.

Notice the second-order effect. As private label compresses commodity tiers, the value of a still-inaccessible brand goes up. But the only buyer with the capital to acquire it is also the one whose distribution machine will reliably destroy what made it worth buying. The acquirers who can hold that contradiction in their hands are the only ones who matter.

Some Acquisitions Have Worked, Not Many, and Not by Accident

The acquisitions that have worked share a single characteristic. The acquirer kept real operational independence for the brand, even when it felt uncomfortable to do so. L'Oréal's 2000 acquisition of Kiehl's is a partial example. The pharmacy brand, founded in 1851, has kept its no-mass-advertising philosophy intact (no billboards, no TV spots), and the stores still feel like the thing people sought out in the first place. Ben & Jerry's is the more extreme case. When Unilever acquired it in 2000, the deal terms enshrined an independent board of directors. That board has publicly clashed with Unilever on the West Bank settlements dispute and on dairy farming standards, sometimes in ways that created real diplomatic problems for the rest of Unilever's portfolio. That's what operational independence actually costs. It's the cost most acquirers refuse to pay. These are exceptions for a reason. They require organizational restraint that runs directly against the financial and supply-chain logic that justified the acquisition in the first place. Most acquirers can't hold that tension for more than a few quarters.

Why Non-Alcoholic Beverages Crystallize Everything

The category that crystallizes every thread of this story (the identity shift, the two strategic paths, the distribution paradox, and a generational consumer change still underappreciated) is non-alcoholic beverages.

Consider what's happened over the last decade. Athletic Brewing was founded in 2017 on the premise that people who don't drink alcohol still want the ritual of beer. By 2024, it was a top-ten US craft brewery by volume without selling a single alcoholic drink, and reportedly valued at ~$800M in its most recent round. The detail that matters most here is the pricing. Athletic priced into the same shelf as premium craft alcohol, not below it, and held that pricing as it scaled. The category orthodoxy was that non-alcoholic beer was a discount play, because the alcohol was the value. Athletic proved that the value was the ritual and the identity. The ABV was kind of incidental. Every other player in the segment is now repricing against that finding.

Liquid Death is the cleaner version of the same insight. The product is water. The valuation, roughly $1.4B as of its 2024 round, is entirely the cultural position. It's a can people can hold at a bar without it announcing they're sober. That's the entire product. The water is just the delivery mechanism. Once you see that, you start seeing how much of the next decade of consumer brand-building is going to look like this. Products engineered around the social shape of an occasion, not the contents of the package.

The lazy pushback is that every generation drinks less in its early twenties. Wait until Gen Z has disposable income and professional stress, and they'll come around. The harder pushback is more interesting and worth taking seriously. The reported drinking decline is strongest in survey environments where social desirability bias rewards the answer, and ground-truth consumption (bar receipts, off-premise volumes, household panel data) may revert closer to historical norms once income and stress arrive in equal measure. That objection deserves an honest answer. And it still misreads what's happening. Two things have changed in a way that isn't cyclical.

Sleep optimization culture (tracking HRV, tracking RHR) has made alcohol's effect on sleep visible to a health-focused young audience in a way it never was before. Mental health awareness has gone up among Gen Z, which has tagged alcohol as a behavioral risk in ways that are fully mainstream now. The signal that matters most isn't behavioral. It's identity. Being sober or sober-curious has become a positive identity marker for Gen Z, not a deprivation. When abstaining from alcohol becomes aspirational, when it's a thing people do because of who they are, not because of what they're avoiding, the change is real, and the change is permanent. The behavioral shift preceded the regulatory pressure. Sugar is seeing it too, with the rise of zero-sugar drinks. The companies building for that reality are already positioned.

Where Larger CPG Players Go From Here

Here's the paradox the rest of this argument has been circling. Acquirers are compelled to keep making these deals. The ability gap is real, the category shift is real, the timing is real. And most of them will execute the deals badly, for the exact reasons I just laid out. Both things stay true. The next five years will produce a wave of acquisitions, and most of those acquisitions will quietly destroy the value they paid for. The alpha sits with the small number of acquirers who have figured out that the distribution machine is a choice, not an inevitability. Everyone else will run the Dollar Shave Club playbook, write it off in five years, and tell their boards it was a culture problem. So what should we expect? My narrowest, most confident prediction is this. Acquisition activity will accelerate in health, wellness, and zero alcohol over the next three to five years, and the deals that get made will be driven by two compounding factors.

The first force is structural, but it isn't a talent problem. AB InBev cannot build Athletic Brewing from a standing start because the constraints that make Athletic possible are the constraints a public CPG can't impose on itself. Innovation pipelines inside large CPGs are optimized for line extensions that ride existing distribution. Brand-ladder logic punishes anything that competes with a flagship. Internal IRR thresholds kill projects with under $200M of year-five revenue ambition. And the slow brand-building that creates cultural authority? The refusal of distribution that would dilute the position? The premium pricing held against promotional pressure? The founder's voice surviving a board meeting? All of that is the exact behavior that gets edited out by the second quarterly review. The community trust, the founder story, the way Athletic kept saying no to distributors even when the deal was already on the calendar; that took years, and you can't engineer it backward inside a public earnings cycle. Acquisition isn't the preferred path. It's the only path.

The ability gap matters more now than at any point in the industry's history because of what's happening underneath. Private labels like Amazon Basics, Kirkland, and store-brand organics have been compressing margins in commodity tiers at a pace that most public CPG analysts still underweight. As the bottom of the portfolio commoditizes into a price war that big CPG can't win, the only available margin sits at the top. And the top is identity-coded. They can't build identity-coded brands organically fast enough. Private label and acquisition are the same story told from opposite ends of the shelf.

The second factor is timing, which is the most underappreciated of the two. Category-defining brands compound at premium multiples the moment a board has visibility into the unit economics. The discount window between proven and priced-in is roughly 18 to 36 months. Long enough to be real, short enough that most acquirers miss it because their own internal approval cycles eat six to nine months. After roughly $300M in revenue with mature distribution, the cultural premium has compressed into the multiple, and the deal stops being accretive. The acquirers who'll actually create value here are the ones who can move at founder pace and still write a check at corporate scale. There aren't many of them, and that's the whole point.

The Acquisitions Worth Watching

Start with David. Peter Rahal co-founded RXBar, sold it to Kellogg's for $600M in 2017, and came back with David. The product itself: 28g of protein in a 150-calorie bar, zero sugar. That macro profile didn't exist at retail before David. The brand went viral on launch in a way almost nothing in shelf-stable food does. Mars is the obvious acquirer for a few reasons. Confectionery is the segment Mars has been quietly de-emphasizing. The Petcare cash flow makes the check writable without forcing a strategic re-rate. And Rahal's RXBar/Kellogg's experience means he will negotiate operational independence into the term sheet on day one, which is the actual unlock. Mars learns from M&A failure faster than its peers because it doesn't have to manage a public earnings cycle.

Magic Spoon goes to Kellanova. Cereal at the volume tier is in real decline. The only available growth is premium-coded, macro-rebuilt cereal aimed at adult Gen Z and millennial buyers. That's exactly what Magic Spoon has spent five years building, and it's a category Kellanova just can't innovate into from a Frosted Flakes starting point. M&A inside cereal is going to look like this. Kellanova buys Magic Spoon to keep General Mills from doing it first, and pays a premium that wouldn't pencil out if you didn't factor in the optionality of not buying it.

LMNT goes to PepsiCo. Gatorade owns the legacy electrolyte category, but every dollar of category growth has migrated to no-sugar, high-sodium, performance-coded formulation. That's LMNT's exact position. The risk PepsiCo can't tolerate is Coca-Cola buying LMNT first and giving BodyArmor an adjacent premium SKU at the high end of the shelf. Defensive timing pressure, plus capability gap, plus founder-led brand authenticity that Gatorade just can't build internally; same playbook as Poppi, 12 months later.

Seed goes to Nestlé Health Science, with Reckitt as a likely secondary bidder. Probiotics is the category where the supplement industry's credibility problem is most acute, and Seed has spent its entire existence (clinical trials, peer-reviewed publications, transparent formulation) distinguishing itself from that problem. For Nestlé Health Science, this is the cleanest available entry into a category they've publicly identified as strategic. Buying Seed is buying the science narrative whole, not just the brand. The risk is timing. Seed is small enough right now to be acquirable on terms that still respect the founder's editorial control, and that window closes the moment the next funding round prices in the category leadership Seed has already earned.

Here's the caveat, and it matters because of the distribution paradox I described earlier. The acquirers that generate value from these deals will be the ones who have figured out that getting a brand to 100,000 doors is a choice, not an inevitability. The ones that treat integration as the goal, instead of protecting the brand's cultural integrity, will repeat the Dollar Shave Club playbook. And honestly, there are a lot of them.

The acquirers are trying to buy their way to an answer. The brands worth acquiring (Liquid Death and whatever comes after it) are the ones that answered the question so clearly that a balance sheet was willing to pay billions just to be in proximity to that clarity. Then the acquiring infrastructure does what acquiring infrastructure is built to do, and the brand stops being the brand.

The category is unstable. The human need underneath isn't. The distance between those two facts is where the most interesting consumer business of the next decade is going to get built. And we're nowhere near finished watching it play out.
