Something has been bothering me about how people talk about the CPG industry. The conversation usually goes one of two ways: either incumbents are doomed because they can't innovate, or they're fine because they have distribution and capital. I don't think either of those is the right frame.

What I keep coming back to is a simpler observation: the categories that defined CPG for a century are becoming unstable. Not disappearing — unstable. And the companies that are navigating this well have figured out something that sounds obvious but is genuinely hard to execute: consumers don't organize their lives around product categories. They organize around identity, moment, and meaning. A person buying Athletic Brewing's non-alcoholic IPA isn't buying "beer without alcohol." They're buying a social ritual and a signal about who they are. The category on the label is almost irrelevant to the decision.

That shift is putting enormous pressure on companies whose entire organizational logic — their R&D, their sales force, their shelf negotiations, their brand architecture — was built around a category that consumers are quietly redefining underneath them. The response, when I look across the industry, has taken two distinct forms. And understanding both — and understanding why one of them has a far worse track record than it should — is the most interesting strategic story in consumer today.

The Companies Reinventing Themselves From the Inside

The first group of companies looked at category instability and decided to bet on a new identity from within. This requires a kind of organizational conviction that's genuinely rare in large public companies — you have to look at a legacy business generating most of your current revenue and decide to move resources away from it before the market forces you to. Most management teams can't do it.

Philip Morris International is the most dramatic example of this in modern corporate history. PMI is a company whose core product is cigarettes. They looked at declining volumes, generational stigma, and tightening regulation and made a bet that "nicotine delivery" — not "cigarettes" — was the actual category they were in. That distinction is everything. IQOS, their heated tobacco device, directly cannibalizes their cigarette volumes. ZYN, the nicotine pouch brand they got through their $16.4 billion acquisition of Swedish Match in 2022, was explicitly bought to accelerate the shift away from their own legacy. Today roughly 38% of their net revenue comes from smoke-free products, and they've committed publicly to being a majority smoke-free company by revenue before 2030.

What makes PMI worth studying isn't just the ambition. It's the discipline. They didn't diversify into food or beverages or pharmaceuticals. They stayed tightly in their lane — nicotine, regulatory navigation, global distribution — and just changed the delivery format. The category they expanded was narrow and coherent. That's what makes it credible rather than panicked diversification.

Mars is subtler but equally interesting. You think of Mars as the company behind Snickers and M&Ms. In 2017 they spent $7.7 billion acquiring VCA, one of America's largest veterinary hospital chains. That sounds random until you realize they'd already been in pet food for decades — Pedigree, Whiskas, Royal Canin. The vet acquisition wasn't diversification. It was a logical extension of a quietly reframed identity: Mars doesn't sell confectionery. Mars serves the emotional relationships humans have — with pleasure, with the animals they love. Their category isn't chocolate. It's care.

Nestlé is doing the same thing, more slowly. They've divested much of their confectionery portfolio since 2018, selling their US candy brands to Ferrero, while aggressively building out coffee (Nespresso, Blue Bottle, a $7.15 billion deal with Starbucks for CPG licensing rights) and a health science division that deliberately sits between food and pharmaceutical. They're not becoming a pharma company. But they're not really a chocolate company anymore either.

What these three companies share is that they've named — explicitly, to investors and internally — what business they're actually in. That sounds simple. It almost never is.

The Companies Buying Their Way Into Relevance

The second group decided that the consumer trust they needed was faster to buy than to build. The logic makes sense: niche brands spend years earning authenticity, community, and cultural relevance in ways that large CPG companies structurally can't replicate quickly. Buy the brand, and you buy the relationship. The large company brings distribution and capital. The acquired brand brings the identity the acquirer has lost.

PepsiCo is executing the most disciplined version of this strategy right now. They bought SodaStream for $3.2 billion in 2018. They acquired Siete Foods — a grain-free, Mexican-American food brand with fierce Gen Z and millennial loyalty — for $1.2 billion in 2024. In 2025 they paid $1.65 billion for Poppi, the prebiotic soda brand built almost entirely around gut health and Gen Z aesthetics. Each deal targeted a consumer segment that Pepsi-Cola and Lay's simply couldn't reach organically. Each bet on structural demand growth rather than a trend.

Unilever and P&G pursued similar strategies with more mixed outcomes. Unilever bought Dollar Shave Club for $1 billion in 2016. They acquired Tatcha, Dermalogica, Paula's Choice — premium skincare DTC brands carrying the consumer relationships Unilever's legacy brands had been slowly losing. P&G bought Native (natural deodorant, $100 million in 2017) and First Aid Beauty. The intent in every case was the same: these brands had earned something that the acquirer couldn't build at the pace the market required.

And then something predictable happened.

Why Acquisitions Keep Failing, and Why It's Not What You Think

The conventional wisdom on why CPG acquisitions of niche brands fail is about culture or talent — the founders leave, the team doesn't fit, corporate processes slow everything down. That's real. But I think the deeper problem is structural, and it's worth naming clearly: the main advantage that a large CPG company brings to an acquisition is the exact thing that destroys what made the brand worth buying.

Large incumbents have distribution and capital. Getting a brand into 60,000 doors in 18 months is a real advantage. Having the balance sheet to fund national media and weather bad quarters matters. But the niche brand's value — the thing that justified a 10x or 15x revenue multiple — was almost entirely a function of its inaccessibility, its specificity, its feeling of discovery. The person who found Native deodorant through a DTC channel in 2017 bought it partly because it wasn't next to Gillette. The day P&G pushes it to Walmart at scale, those consumers register the shift. The product hasn't changed. The meaning has. And meaning is often all a niche brand ever was.

Dollar Shave Club is the clearest example. Unilever paid $1 billion for what was, honestly, a media company that happened to make razors. Michael Dubin's original viral video wasn't marketing for a product — it was the product. Within three years of the acquisition, Dubin was gone, the brand's content had been sanitized into something indistinguishable from every other CPG campaign, and subscription growth had stalled. Unilever sold Dollar Shave Club in 2023 to Nexus Capital Management for a fraction of what they paid. The brand still exists. The thing that made it worth a billion dollars doesn't.

Bolthouse Farms followed the same arc. Campbell Soup paid $1.55 billion in 2012 and sold it to private equity at a significant loss by 2019 — the business couldn't integrate cleanly into Campbell's operational model without losing the agility that made it relevant. Annie's, acquired by General Mills for $820 million in 2014, still sits on shelves — but its identity has been steadily diluted as General Mills applied its standard promotional playbook to a brand that was built on clean-label credibility and community authenticity.

The pattern is consistent: the acquiring company brings the brand into its distribution infrastructure because that's the entire point, and the infrastructure optimizes for scale, velocity, and promotional efficiency. Those are exactly the forces the niche brand was built in opposition to. Rapid distribution signals "arrival" to mainstream consumers while telling the early adopter community that the brand no longer belongs to them. Premium pricing gets eroded by promotional mechanics. The acquisition destroys the very clarity it was trying to purchase.

The acquisitions that have worked — and they exist — share one characteristic: the acquirer maintained genuine operational independence for the brand, even when that was uncomfortable. Diageo's acquisition of Seedlip kept the brand largely separate from Diageo's mainstream operations. Hormel's acquisition of Justin's Nut Butter outperformed most comparable deals because they resisted the urge to fully integrate it. These are the exceptions. They require organizational restraint that runs directly against the financial logic that justified the acquisition in the first place. Most acquirers can't hold that tension for long.

Zero-Alcohol Is Where Everything Converges

If I had to pick one category that crystallizes every thread of this story — the category identity shift, the two strategic paths, the distribution paradox, and a generational consumer change that I think is still underappreciated — it's non-alcoholic beverages.

Consider what's happened in eight years. Athletic Brewing Company, founded in 2017 on the premise that people who don't drink alcohol still want the ritual of beer, is now a top-ten US craft brewery by volume. Without selling a single alcoholic drink. They've raised north of $50 million, are reportedly valued around $800 million, and have demonstrated that non-alcoholic can support genuine brand loyalty at premium price points — something the industry dismissed as implausible a decade ago.

Liquid Death sells canned water with heavy metal branding and "murder your thirst" as its tagline. It's valued at $1.4 billion. The product is water. The value is entirely the cultural position the brand occupies — a can-in-hand social ritual for people who don't want to drink alcohol but also don't want to hold a sparkling water that signals they're not drinking alcohol. There's something clever happening there that I think most people in the industry have been slow to take seriously.

Olipop grew from $4 million to over $200 million in annual revenue in roughly five years. Poppi, its closest competitor in the functional soda space, was acquired by PepsiCo for $1.65 billion in 2025. That deal alone should have ended the debate about whether this is a trend or a category. PepsiCo doesn't pay $1.65 billion for trends.

The incumbents have been paying attention. Heineken 0.0 is one of the fastest-growing SKUs in Heineken's global portfolio. AB InBev is building non-alcoholic versions across its brand portfolio. Diageo has been making noise around "mindful drinking" for years. They all understand that they need to own this shelf space before someone else does.

Is Gen Z's Relationship With Alcohol Actually Different?

The obvious pushback on all of this is: every generation drinks less in their early twenties. Wait until Gen Z has disposable income and professional stress. They'll find alcohol just fine.

I think that misreads what's actually happening, and the reasons matter for how you think about the investment thesis here.

Cannabis is now legal in more than half of US states and functions as a direct substitute for alcohol in many social contexts. That's not anecdotal — the correlation between cannabis legalization and reduced alcohol consumption in those markets is documented. Mental health awareness among Gen Z has specifically tagged alcohol as a behavioral risk in ways that have gone fully mainstream in their culture. "Hangxiety" is a widely understood concept in this cohort in a way it simply wasn't for millennials at the same age. Sleep optimization culture — Whoop, Oura Ring, tracking HRV — has made alcohol's effect on sleep viscerally visible to health-focused young people in a way it never was before.

But the signal that matters most to me isn't behavioral — it's identity. Being sober or "sober curious" has become a positive identity marker for Gen Z rather than a deprivation. When abstaining from alcohol becomes aspirational — a thing people do because of who they are, not because of what they're avoiding — the change is structural, not cyclical. That's what happened to smoking among educated consumers in the 1990s. The behavioral shift preceded the regulatory pressure. I think the same dynamic is underway with alcohol now.

This doesn't mean alcohol disappears. It means the category contracts and premiumizes — fewer occasions, higher quality, more intentional consumption. Fewer but better drinks. The companies building for that reality are already positioned. The ones treating zero-alcohol as a line extension rather than a category are running out of time.

Where I Think the M&A Goes From Here

The prediction I'm most confident in is narrow but specific: acquisition activity will accelerate in health, wellness, and zero-alcohol over the next three to five years — and the deals that get made will be driven by three compounding pressures.

The first is the capability gap. AB InBev cannot build a brand that looks and feels like Athletic Brewing from a standing start. The community trust, the founder story, the distribution restraint that made Athletic what it is — these took years and can't be manufactured. The only path for incumbents is acquisition, and they know it.

The second is private label. Amazon, Kirkland, and store-brand organic products have been commoditizing CPG categories at a pace that's accelerated significantly. The categories most defensible against private label are the ones where genuine brand identity creates sustainable price premium. Acquirers are increasingly trying to buy that defense before they need it.

The third is timing. The best zero-alcohol and functional beverage brands are currently small enough that their cultural identity is intact and their price is manageable. Three to five years from now, the ones that survive will either have gone public or will have been acquired. The window to buy at a defensible price, with founder integrity intact, is closing.

The specific names I'd watch: Athletic Brewing is the most obvious acquisition target in the category — Heineken and AB InBev are best positioned strategically. Lyre's, the non-alcoholic spirits brand operating in over 50 countries, represents the premium spirits play. In functional beverages, the next Poppi-scale acquisition will likely come from the adaptogens space — brands like Recess, Hiyo, or De La Calle are building the category now. Watch for the acquisition announcements in 2027 and 2028.

The caveat — and it matters — is the distribution paradox I described earlier. The acquirers that generate value from these deals will be the ones who've internalized that getting a brand into 60,000 doors is a choice, not an inevitability. The ones that treat integration as the goal rather than the brand's ongoing cultural integrity will repeat the Dollar Shave Club playbook. And there are a lot of them.

What I Keep Coming Back To

The CPG companies that will matter in 2035 are the ones that have answered one question clearly: what human need do we actually serve, and how broadly can we define the category around that need without losing what makes us credible?

PMI's answer is nicotine ritual. Mars's answer is the love people have for the things that matter to them. Nestlé is still working out its answer, which is part of why its transformation has been messier than the other two. The acquirers — PepsiCo most precisely, Unilever with the most variable execution — are trying to buy their way to an answer. The risk is that the acquisition becomes the answer when what it should be is evidence of the answer.

The brands being acquired — Athletic Brewing, Liquid Death, whatever comes next — are the ones that answered the question so clearly that a company was willing to pay hundreds of millions of dollars just to be in proximity to that clarity. The tragedy, repeated often enough to feel like a pattern, is that the acquisition frequently destroys the clarity it was trying to purchase.

The category is unstable. The human need underneath it isn't. The distance between those two facts is where the most interesting business-building is happening right now — and I don't think it's close to being fully played out.
